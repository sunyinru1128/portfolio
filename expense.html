project OVERVIEW

Since Oct 2020, I have been working on the financial service system in ByteDance, one of the biggest internet company in China. Joining as a full-time UX designer, I took a design project from 0 to 1 that provides efficient and seamless financial support for more than 100 thousand worldwide staff.

The project includes 3 end-to-end products, which have been launched, internationalized and commercialized.

Design goal

Create an efficient and secure expense experience

Imagine being an employee waiting for reimbursement after covering a company expense. Your primary thought? ‚ÄúReimburse me quickly and securely!‚Äù Recognizing the dual necessity for efficiency and security we delved deep into understanding stakeholder dynamics.

Strategy # 1

Bridge the Experience Gap to promote smooth journey

Featured Case: Optimize user flows in expense report O2O management

Research

Users do not know whether, when and how to submit receipts

During our beta testing, I spearheaded usability tests with 24 users and conducted interviews to gather insights. Based on the research, I found that many of their experience gap are concentrated in one phase: After they complete online expense reports, they do not know whether, when, and how to submit receipts.

Define design objective

Build process awareness and help users with clear guidance

Based on the research, I focused the design scope on the ‚Äúafter-submission‚Äù phase. 

Design

How to find the design opportunities?

Highlight1: HMW Reframing the flow from the user's perspective

The division of the silos makes sense to the business units, but makes no sense to the customer, who sees the entire offering as one experience. ( From ‚ÄòService Design‚Äô, Andy Polaine)

Before: Roughly mix up the backstage logic to users

Users should see a cohesive, seamless experience rather than fragmented business processes. Through service design, I discerned the underlying complexity presented to the users, such as the stage like ‚ÄúUnder processing, receipt submitting‚Äù which were too confusing.

After: Reconstruct front stage flow in ‚Äúuser language‚Äù

I reorganized the user flow by transforming complex backstage logic into user-perspective flow, which is easier to understand and follow. In this way, users could clearly know what to focus on in different stages.

Highlight2: HMW Visualize the step line to create user expectation

By doing competitive analysis among similar financial product, I found most interactions are moderate. As a result, I do some cross-border product analysis to seek more inspirations.

Here is an interesting finding: The scenario of E-commerce products returns is similar to my targeted scenario to some extent:

Taking inspiration from e-commerce returns by ‚Äòscenario analogy‚Äô, I wondered: How might we visualize each step for clarity?

We introduced ‚Äòstep-by-step‚Äô interactions, fostering user control and clearer expectations for tasks like receipt printing and submission. Besides, scenario-based guidance, differentiated copy guidelines for personalized scenarios

Strategy # 2

Simplify the flow stages to maximize efficiency

Featured Case: OCR table filling

User problem

Massive manual input is a nightmare

‚ÄòIt kills me to input so many information into the system line by line, costing more than an hour to complete this task‚Äô (From one user)

Given the rigor of the financial system, our original reimbursement tools require users to manually fill in lots of items in the system for the company to review. It takes a lot of time for users to locate and input the required code to the system from the densely packed receipts back and forth.

Inspiration

Think outside of the ‚Äútable sheet‚Äù

Our current flow was daunting, with users navigating 11+ steps, inputting around 30 form items, often repetitively. ‚ÄúFilling the table sheet‚Äù seems to be a ‚Äúcommon solution‚Äù for many products for business. But, what if we automate the whole process by leveraging technology like OCR (Optical character recognition) identification to cover some of manual efforts insteadÔºü

A-HA moment

What if we use OCR to automate expense reports completion?

Iteration

Align technology with user cognitive behaviors

To create an enjoyable and seamless journey, we kept collecting 10+ OCR products on the market, categorized by 4 scenarios and analyzed their product features and interactions throughout the ideation and iteration process, shedding light on our solution under the reimbursement context.

After we completed the demo and tested on users, we found out that users do save lots of time by scanning and automatically filling in forms instead of manually completing every item. However, we also found some usability problems because of tech limitation. The OCR recognition is not always accurate, affected by factors like environmental lighting, clarity of receipts, etc. As a result, we need to make further improvement to solve these problems:

‚ÄòAfter taking the photo of receipt it shows some errors, and it took me some time to verify and revise all the items on the form‚Äô (From one tester)

impact

Automate with technology to make reimbursement easy

In the original system, users need to fill in lots of repeated and complicated tables back and forth. But now, they just need to scan the receipt, check and upload, five steps at minimum to make online report filling fast and easy. Originally they need to spend at least 30 mins on submitting one expense report, now they only need to spend 10 mins on the whole task, which increase 67% time on reimbursement.

Strategy # 3

Solve complicated problems from system thinking

Featured Case: Transfer cost from staff to financial reviewers

User problem

Blocking points that receipts cannot be reimbursed for the day

A significant 16.7% of users were blocked when trying to reimburse same-day receipts, due to financial audit concerns.

Problem analysis

Balance different stakeholder‚Äôs needs

To decode the root cause, I evaluated stakeholder needs and realized that the constraints stemmed from financial auditors‚Äô concerns. In China, validating a VAT receipt entails a thorough check within the China‚Äôs State Taxation Administration (STA), sometimes requiring a day. Therefore, auditors hope to set aside a day to ensure that the uploaded receipts have passed STA verification.

By digging into the problem, I found out the key is to pry out a balanced product experience between different stakeholders.

Common Staff

I need easy and fast reimbursement

Financial Auditor

I need to prevent archiving invalid receipts

Challenge

How to balance the need conflicts between stakeholders?

HMW ideation

So, HMW shift the burden of receipt checking from common staff side to the financial auditory side?

According to the Tesler‚Äôs Law (The Law of Conservation of Complexity), for any system there is a certain amount of complexity which cannot be reduced. All processes have a core of complexity that cannot be designed away and therefore must be assumed by either the system or the user. Ensure as much as possible of the burden is lifted from users by dealing with inherent complexity during design and development.

Remove hindrances on the common staff side of the reimbursement process by allowing the expense reports including today‚Äôs receipts to be submitted

Add a new auditory point without affecting efficiency: For instance, If the receipts haven‚Äôt been checked by STA, highlighted message to guide auditors to pend and review it later. 

reflection

Keep the ‚Äúright balance‚Äù of multiple stakeholders in a system

During the construction of the financial system, I found it hard to achieve ‚Äúwin-win‚Äù for all stakeholders. The three main roles, common staff, financial auditors and team leaders, often ‚Äúconflict‚Äù with each other. For instance, as in the third case, the ‚Äúreceipt STA review‚Äù process benefits financial side for avoiding financial risk, but lower the efficiency for common staff. As a result, it is critical to balance between different stakeholders in the system to maximize the overall product effects.
